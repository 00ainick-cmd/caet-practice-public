# Self-Determination Theory

## One-line claim

Treat motivation as a function of three innate psychological needs — autonomy (volition over one's actions), competence (effective interaction with the environment), and relatedness (caring connection with others) — and design every tutor move to support, not thwart, all three.

## Evidence base

Self-Determination Theory (SDT) is a macro-theory of human motivation organized around three innate psychological needs: autonomy, competence, and relatedness. Deci and Ryan's (2000) target article in *Psychological Inquiry* synthesized roughly two decades of laboratory and field work to argue that the satisfaction of these three needs is the operative mechanism distinguishing autonomous motivation (intrinsic motivation and well-internalized extrinsic motivation) from controlled motivation (external regulation and introjected regulation), and that autonomous motivation predicts deeper learning, persistence, and well-being while controlled motivation predicts compliance and short-horizon performance only (Deci & Ryan, 2000, pp. 229-237). Ryan and Deci (2000) translated this into the canonical *American Psychologist* framework that mapped need-supportive social conditions onto motivational quality and downstream outcomes across education, work, sport, and health.

Modern meta-analytic evidence places the operational claims on solid ground. Deci, Koestner, and Ryan's (1999) meta-analysis of 128 experiments in *Psychological Bulletin* established that tangible rewards contingent on engagement, completion, or performance significantly *undermine* free-choice intrinsic motivation (d = -0.40, -0.36, -0.28 respectively); positive verbal feedback, by contrast, enhances it (d = +0.33). Su and Reeve (2011) meta-analyzed 19 autonomy-support intervention studies in *Educational Psychology Review* and reported a weighted effect size of d = 0.63 — autonomy-supportive instruction is teachable and the gain transfers to learner motivation, engagement, and achievement. Cerasoli, Nicklin, and Ford (2014) reviewed 183 studies (N = 212,468) in *Psychological Bulletin* and reported intrinsic motivation as a medium-to-strong predictor of performance (ρ = 0.21-0.45 across school, work, and physical domains), with the relationship holding whether or not extrinsic incentives were present and strongest on quality (vs. quantity) outcomes. Ryan and Deci's (2020) educational synthesis in *Contemporary Educational Psychology* operationalized the three needs as classroom moves and confirmed cross-cultural generality.

Two boundary conditions are well-documented. First, autonomy support is not laissez-faire: Vansteenkiste, Ryan, and Soenens (2020) emphasized in *Motivation and Emotion* that autonomy support and structure are independent dimensions, and the largest gains come from autonomy-supportive *structured* environments — clear expectations, scaffolded practice, and informational feedback delivered in a non-controlling voice (pp. 12-15). Second, the rewards finding has nuance: unexpected rewards, task-noncontingent rewards, and verbal feedback do not undermine intrinsic motivation; the effect is specific to expected, tangible, contingent rewards on initially interesting tasks (Deci, Koestner, & Ryan, 1999, pp. 653-656).

## When to apply

- **Learner engagement is dropping mid-unit** — disengagement, perfunctory responses, or
  expressed boredom signal need-frustration. Diagnose which need is unmet (autonomy: "I have
  no say"; competence: "I keep failing"; relatedness: "no one cares whether I learn this")
  before choosing the move.
- **An extrinsic reward decision is pending** — before adding badges, leaderboards, points,
  or contingent payment to an initially interesting task, apply Deci, Koestner, & Ryan
  (1999): expected-tangible-contingent rewards reliably undermine intrinsic motivation.
- **New unit kickoff** — provide a meaningful rationale for why this content matters,
  surface a defensible choice (sub-topic, order, example), and connect the unit to a
  longer-horizon goal the learner has named. This sets the autonomy and competence anchors.
- **Choice point is available without compromising the objective** — when two or more
  instructionally-equivalent paths exist (problem A vs. B, video vs. text, paired vs. solo),
  surfacing the choice is a near-zero-cost autonomy-support move (Niemiec & Ryan, 2009).
- **Feedback delivery required** — phrase feedback as informational ("here is what worked,
  here is the gap, here is the next step") rather than evaluative ("good job" / "wrong").
  Informational feedback supports competence; evaluative feedback risks introjection (Ryan
  & Deci, 2000, pp. 70-72).
- **Long-horizon retention or transfer is the criterion** — autonomous motivation predicts
  deep learning, persistence, and transfer better than controlled motivation (Cerasoli et
  al., 2014); when the program runs longer than one session, invest in need support.

## When NOT to apply

- **First exposure to high-element-interactivity content** — when the learner has no schema
  yet, asking for choice or self-direction increases extraneous load and frustrates
  competence. Provide a worked example or guided explanation first
  ([worked-example-effect](../01-learning-science/worked-example-effect.md)), then layer
  autonomy support as the schema stabilizes
  ([expertise-reversal](../01-learning-science/expertise-reversal.md)).
- **Material is genuinely uninteresting and the horizon is short** — Ryan and Deci (2020)
  note that for short, low-stakes compliance tasks (one-time safety acknowledgement), full
  SDT need-support has marginal payoff vs. cost. Provide an honest rationale and complete
  the task; do not force a participation structure the task does not warrant.
- **Autonomy support is being confused with permissiveness** — removing structure, lowering
  expectations, or letting the learner skip difficult work is *not* autonomy support and
  predicts worse outcomes (Vansteenkiste et al., 2020, pp. 12-15). If the move reduces
  structure rather than reframing it, it is a structure failure, not an SDT application.
- **External regulation is appropriate to the context** — compliance training, regulatory
  updates, and onboarding tasks are well-served by clear external requirements; forcing
  internalization risks introjection without producing autonomous motivation.
- **Reward already delivered as part of an expected contingency on an interesting task** —
  once the learner has been operating under "do X to get Y," removing the reward will not
  restore baseline intrinsic motivation in the short term (Deci, Koestner, & Ryan, 1999).
  Stabilize need support and let the relationship rebuild; do not over-correct.

## How to apply

- **Provide a meaningful rationale for every required activity** — when a task cannot be
  chosen away from, explain *why* it matters in terms the learner can connect to their own
  goals. A 20-second rationale converts external regulation toward identified regulation
  (Niemiec & Ryan, 2009) at near-zero cost.
- **Surface defensible choices, not cosmetic ones** — choice over which problem to solve
  next, which sub-topic to tackle first, or which medium to use supports autonomy when the
  options are instructionally equivalent. Cosmetic choice (avatar color, badge name)
  produces no measurable need satisfaction and signals decoration was mistaken for autonomy.
- **Use informational, not evaluative, feedback** — "your trace shows the squitter at 1090
  MHz but the framing pulses are 50% low — check the cable connection next" is
  informational; "good work" or "wrong" is evaluative. Informational feedback supports
  competence; evaluative feedback signals judgment and invites introjection (Ryan & Deci,
  2000). See [error-analysis-corrective-feedback](../04-delivery-patterns/error-analysis-corrective-feedback.md).
- **Pair autonomy support with structure, not in tension with it** — clear expectations,
  scaffolded practice, and predictable feedback are competence supports; delivering them in
  a non-controlling voice ("here is the next step you can take" rather than "you must do
  this") makes the same structure autonomy-supportive (Vansteenkiste et al., 2020, pp. 12-15).
- **Default to verbal recognition over tangible rewards on interesting tasks** — Deci,
  Koestner, & Ryan (1999) found verbal positive feedback enhances intrinsic motivation
  (d = +0.33) while expected tangible rewards undermine it (d = -0.36). On an
  already-engaging task, recognition does the work of a reward without the cost.
- **Build relatedness through specific noticing** — "I noticed you tried a different decoder
  ordering this round — what was the reasoning?" signals the tutor is paying attention to
  *this* learner. Generic praise ("great job!") fails the relatedness test; specific,
  interest-led noticing satisfies it. See [voice-style-agency-preserving-language](../05-tutor-personas/voice-style-agency-preserving-language.md).

## Common misapplications

- **Equating autonomy support with removing structure** — letting the learner skip the
  difficult problem, lowering the criterion, or omitting feedback is not autonomy support
  and predicts worse outcomes (Vansteenkiste et al., 2020). The structure stays; the
  *voice* changes.
- **Adding gamification points to a task learners already find interesting** — points,
  badges, and leaderboards are tangible, expected, and contingent — the conditions Deci,
  Koestner, & Ryan (1999) identified as undermining (d = -0.36). On interesting tasks they
  erode the motivation that was already there.
- **Treating "choice" as a checkbox** — surfacing a choice between equivalent cosmetics
  while withholding choice over substantive aspects of the work is autonomy theatre, and
  learners notice. Either give the choice substantively or do not pretend.
- **Replacing evaluative feedback with empty praise** — "good job!" with no information
  about what worked, why, or what comes next is evaluative feedback in a softer voice, not
  informational feedback. Competence support requires the model-update, not the affect.
- **Forcing internalization on tasks with genuinely external value** — pretending a
  regulatory acknowledgement is intrinsically meaningful invites skepticism. Provide the
  honest rationale ("this is required because…") rather than performing motivation.

## Examples across domains

**Avionics — kicking off a four-week ADS-B Out installation unit with a mid-career technician.**

*Setup.* The technician has done VHF nav installs and basic transponder work but never a
full ADS-B Out compliance install on a new airframe. The unit covers wiring, configuration,
ramp testing, and FAA logbook documentation. The SDT move treats unit kickoff as a
need-anchoring moment rather than "here is the syllabus, start with chapter 1."

*Need-supportive moves.* The tutor names the rationale ("ADS-B Out is the install your shop
gets the most call-outs on this year — you'll lead the next two; this unit is the fastest
path to running them solo"), surfaces a defensible sequencing choice ("start with the wiring
topology or the configuration software — which feels less familiar?"), and frames feedback
discipline up front ("anything I tell you on a check will be specific — what worked, what's
off, what to check next — never just 'right' or 'wrong'"). All three needs land: autonomy
via rationale and choice, competence via the structure preview, relatedness via specificity.

*Reward discipline.* The shop's existing badging program offers an "ADS-B specialist" pin on
unit completion — per Deci, Koestner, & Ryan (1999) an expected, tangible, contingent reward
on intrinsically interesting work. The tutor cannot remove the pin (shop policy) but
downplays it during the unit, anchors recognition on specific informational feedback ("the
way you re-routed the antenna coax to clear the crossbar is the move that prevents the
install from coming back"), and saves the pin for the unit end without contingent framing.
Expected effect per Su & Reeve (2011): maintained intrinsic motivation (d = 0.63 weighted).

**L2 (second-language) acquisition — adult learner re-engaging with Spanish after a four-month plateau.**

*Setup.* A self-directed adult has been on Duolingo and a textbook for nine months, hit B1,
and stalled. Streaks are intact but engagement is performative — five-minute daily sessions
on easy review items, no production practice, no actual Spanish in the wild. The plateau is
a need-frustration signal: autonomy collapsed (the streak now drives behavior), competence
stalled (review items too easy to produce gain), relatedness absent (no interlocutors).

*Need-supportive moves.* The tutor diagnoses and rebuilds. Autonomy: "the streak isn't your
goal — what is? When do you want to use this?" The learner names "ordering and small talk
on a planned trip in six months." Competence: graduate from review items to scaffolded
production — three short voice messages per week with informational feedback on one specific
feature each (preterite agreement, gender concord, vocabulary precision), not a global
score. Relatedness: a once-a-week 20-minute conversation partner via italki on the learner's
actual hobby, not a textbook topic. The streak becomes optional structure, not the driver.

*Reward discipline.* Duolingo's gem and league system is the canonical Deci, Koestner, &
Ryan (1999) configuration on intrinsically interesting work; for a learner escaping a
plateau, the gamification is now part of the problem. The tutor does not require quitting
the app but reframes its role to "warm-up only — production with the conversation partner is
where the gain comes from." Per Cerasoli, Nicklin, & Ford (2014) (ρ = 0.21-0.45 intrinsic
motivation → performance, strongest on quality) and Su & Reeve (2011), production accuracy
and reported autonomous motivation should rise within four to six weeks.

## Quality signal

The AI tutor knows SDT moves are landing when (a) self-reported autonomous-motivation indices (e.g., the Self-Regulation Questionnaire, Ryan & Deci 2020 review) rise across the unit while controlled-motivation indices fall, (b) voluntary engagement metrics — sessions beyond the required minimum, optional practice attempted, questions asked — increase by ≥30% over baseline, and (c) on a delayed transfer task at +14 days, performance gain correlates with autonomous-motivation change at r > 0.3 (Cerasoli, Nicklin, & Ford 2014 range). A negative signal: rising compliance metrics (streaks, completed assignments) with flat or declining voluntary engagement indicates the tutor is recruiting controlled regulation, not autonomous motivation.
