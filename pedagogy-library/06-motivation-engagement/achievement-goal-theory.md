# Achievement Goal Theory

## One-line claim

Frame feedback and assessment around mastery-approach goals — improvement against the criterion and against one's own prior attempt — rather than performance-approach goals — outperforming peers — when the criterion is durable learning, and reserve performance-approach framing for tasks where the strategy is already known and competitive comparison is the actual job demand.

## Evidence base

Achievement Goal Theory (AGT) distinguishes the *purpose* a learner adopts in an achievement situation: a mastery goal targets developing competence against a self- or task-referenced standard, while a performance goal targets demonstrating competence against a normative (peer) standard. Ames (1992) provided the foundational classroom analysis in *Journal of Educational Psychology*, arguing that the task, evaluation/recognition, and authority structures the instructor controls collectively cue mastery- versus performance-orientation in the learner, and that mastery-orienting structures (interesting tasks, criterion-referenced grading, private feedback, learner choice) produce deeper strategy use, more challenge-seeking, and more persistence than performance-orienting structures (competition, public ranking, ability-emphasizing recognition) (Ames, 1992, pp. 263-268). Elliot and McGregor (2001) refined the construct in *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology* by crossing mastery/performance with approach/avoidance into a 2×2 framework — mastery-approach, mastery-avoidance, performance-approach, performance-avoidance — and showed across three studies that the four constructs are factor-analytically distinct and predict different antecedents and consequences (Elliot & McGregor, 2001, pp. 504-512).

Modern meta-analytic evidence narrows the operational claim. Hulleman, Schrager, Bodmann, and Harackiewicz (2010) synthesized 243 self-report studies (N = 91,087) in *Psychological Bulletin* and showed that not all mastery-approach measures are equivalent — items that emphasize task-improvement standards behave differently than items that emphasize personal-development standards — which forces precision about *which* mastery framing is operationalized (Hulleman et al., 2010, pp. 432-441). Van Yperen, Blaga, and Postmes (2014) meta-analyzed 295 effect sizes (N = 33,983) across work, sport, and education in *PLOS ONE* and found approach goals (mastery-approach and performance-approach) positively predict non-self-report performance and avoidance goals (mastery-avoidance and performance-avoidance) negatively predict it; the mastery-approach to performance link was robust and most pronounced in the work domain (Van Yperen et al., 2014, pp. 4-9). Senko, Hulleman, and Harackiewicz (2011) synthesized the on-the-ground decision rules in *Educational Psychologist*: mastery-approach reliably promotes interest, deep processing, persistence after failure, and adaptive help-seeking; performance-approach reliably promotes graded performance on closed-task assessments where success means matching a normative criterion (Senko, Hulleman, & Harackiewicz, 2011, pp. 28-35).

Two boundary conditions limit naive "mastery is always better" application. First, Senko and Miles (2008) showed in *Contemporary Educational Psychology* that mastery-oriented students disproportionately allocate study effort to personally interesting material and underprepare for boring-but-tested content, which in their path analysis predicted *lower* exam grades — a finding the field calls the "learning agenda" trap (Senko & Miles, 2008, pp. 568-576). Second, Senko and Hulleman (2013) demonstrated in *Journal of Educational Psychology* that goals of either type are abandoned when the learner perceives them as hard to attain; the harder a mastery- or performance-approach goal looked, the less learners pursued it, and the worse interest and achievement became (Senko & Hulleman, 2013, pp. 510-518). The runtime implication is twofold: mastery framing must be coupled to the criterion the assessment will actually score, and goal difficulty must be calibrated to the upper edge of attainable, not aspirational.

## When to apply

- **Session start with prerequisite skill in place** — Open the session by stating the
  mastery-approach criterion ("by end of segment, you can correctly trace all three Mode S
  replies and explain each failure mode") rather than a normative standard ("score in the
  top 10%"). Ames (1992, pp. 263-265) treats opening framing as a primary TARGET lever.
- **Feedback event after a learner attempt** — When delivering feedback, anchor it to the
  learner's own prior attempt and to the criterion, not to peer distribution. Mastery framing
  preserves persistence after failure (Senko, Hulleman, & Harackiewicz, 2011, pp. 30-32).
- **Assessment returned with a mixed result** — On a partial-success result, frame next steps
  as criterion gaps to close ("the two ramp-test indicators you missed"), not as a rank deficit.
- **Error observed mid-task** — Treat the error as diagnostic information ("what did this tell
  you about your model?") rather than evidence of standing relative to others; this preserves
  approach motivation under failure.
- **Segment boundary on novel complex content** — On unfamiliar material, the mastery frame
  protects against the avoidance shift that performance framing produces under uncertainty
  (Elliot & McGregor, 2001, pp. 506-510).
- **Long-horizon retention or transfer is the criterion** — Mastery-approach predicts deeper
  processing and better transfer than performance-approach (Senko, Hulleman, & Harackiewicz,
  2011, pp. 32-35); choose mastery framing when the downstream test is days or weeks out.

## When NOT to apply

- **Initial exposure to high-element-interactivity material** — Any goal frame that demands
  self-evaluation against a criterion adds self-regulatory load to an already-saturated working
  memory (see [cognitive-load-theory](../01-learning-science/cognitive-load-theory.md)). Build
  the schema with worked examples first; introduce mastery framing once a stable model exists.
- **Learner is visibly overwhelmed** — A perceived-hard mastery goal is abandoned just as
  readily as a hard performance goal (Senko & Hulleman, 2013, pp. 514-518). Stabilize load
  first; framing on top of overload triggers withdrawal, not engagement.
- **Strategy is unknown and the task is genuinely novel** — Mastery framing presumes the
  learner can recognize progress against the criterion. When the criterion itself has not been
  taught, drop the goal frame and teach the criterion first (cf. Seijts & Latham 2005 in
  [goal-setting-theory](../06-motivation-engagement/goal-setting-theory.md)).
- **Open-ended exploratory or creative inquiry** — When the desired outcome is divergent
  generation, a fixed mastery criterion narrows the search space prematurely. Use process-level
  prompts instead of an outcome-criterion frame.
- **Closed assessment where outperforming peers is the actual job demand** — Some criteria
  (selection exams, graded-curve certifications) are normatively scored. Performance-approach
  framing can be honest and useful here — but pair it with mastery-approach framing on the
  *learning* phase to protect deep processing (Senko, Hulleman, & Harackiewicz, 2011, pp.
  35-38).
- **Mastery framing is decoupled from the assessment criterion** — When mastery framing lets
  the learner pursue a personal "learning agenda" disconnected from what will be tested, the
  Senko and Miles (2008, pp. 568-576) trap engages and grades fall.

## How to apply

- **Default to mastery-approach framing on feedback and assessment** — "Compared to your last
  attempt, you traced two of three replies correctly; the missed one is the failure-mode
  indicator we covered. What's the next attempt?" Anchors the learner to criterion and prior
  self, not peer rank (Ames, 1992, pp. 263-265).
- **Couple mastery framing to the assessed criterion explicitly** — State the mastery target as
  the same observable that the assessment scores. This forecloses the Senko–Miles (2008) trap
  where the learner pursues their own preferred sub-topic and underprepares the tested one.
- **Avoid normative comparison in routine feedback** — Public ranking, peer-percentile
  framings, and ability-emphasizing praise ("you're so good at this") cue performance
  orientation; replace with effort/strategy attributions tied to the criterion
  ([coach-encourager](../05-tutor-personas/coach-encourager.md)).
- **Use approach framing, not avoidance** — "Demonstrate the three diagnostic moves" beats
  "don't make the three errors." Avoidance framing predicts withdrawal and shallow processing
  across both mastery and performance variants (Elliot & McGregor, 2001, pp. 510-516).
- **Calibrate perceived attainability to ~60-80% expected success** — Senko and Hulleman
  (2013, pp. 510-518) found pursuit collapses as perceived difficulty rises; align mastery
  targets with [zpd-operationalization](../07-runtime-decisions/zpd-operationalization.md)
  rather than with aspiration.
- **On the assessment itself, use criterion-referenced scoring with a fixed threshold** —
  Pair mastery framing with the [mastery-thresholds](../07-runtime-decisions/mastery-thresholds.md)
  and [mastery-learning](../02-instructional-design/mastery-learning.md) structures that score
  against criterion, not curve.
- **Restate the goal as a learning goal on novel complex tasks** — When the strategy is not yet
  stable, frame the target as strategy-discovery, not outcome — same move as
  [goal-setting-theory](../06-motivation-engagement/goal-setting-theory.md), executed in the
  mastery dialect.

## Common misapplications

- **Generic "do your best" framing labeled mastery-approach** — A vague encouragement is
  neither mastery nor performance framing; it has no operative criterion (cf.
  [goal-setting-theory](../06-motivation-engagement/goal-setting-theory.md)). Mastery framing
  requires a specific task-referenced standard.
- **Mastery framing with normative consequences attached** — "Focus on improvement" while the
  posted leaderboard ranks the class injects a performance-orientation cue that overrides the
  framing (Ames, 1992, pp. 264-266). Align all TARGET dimensions or accept the dominant cue
  wins.
- **Praising ability instead of strategy or effort** — "You're naturally good at this"
  produces performance orientation and avoidance motivation under failure; "you used the
  cross-check the procedure called for" produces mastery orientation. The signal is *what
  attribution* the praise installs.
- **Mastery framing that drifts from the assessment criterion** — Senko and Miles (2008, pp.
  568-576): mastery-oriented students who follow their interest neglect tested-but-boring
  topics. Anchor mastery framing to the criterion the assessment will score.
- **Treating mastery vs. performance as a stable trait** — AGT operates on situationally cued *states*; the runtime intervenes on the cue, not on a personality.
- **Setting mastery goals so high learners disengage** — Senko and Hulleman (2013, pp.
  514-518): perceived-impossible mastery goals are abandoned identically to perceived-impossible
  performance goals. Calibrate, do not aspire.

## Examples across domains

**Avionics — Mode S transponder ramp-test feedback during ADS-B Out troubleshooting.**

*Setup.* A technician runs an IFR-6000 ramp test on an installed GTX 345R during a
post-installation functional check. The previous attempt failed: the ES squitter was correct
but the SIL/SDA fields decoded as 0/0 instead of 3/2. The conventional move is "you got 60%,
the others passed first try."

*Mastery-approach framing.* The tutor anchors feedback to criterion and prior self: "Compared
to your last attempt, the squitter is now clean — that's the change since this morning. The
remaining gap is SIL/SDA: 3/2 is the criterion for AC 20-165B compliance. What configuration
register controls those bits, and what's your next move?" The criterion is named, the
learner's own progress is the comparison, and the prompt is approach-framed (demonstrate the
fix), not avoidance-framed (don't fail again). Per Senko, Hulleman, and Harackiewicz (2011,
pp. 30-32), this preserves persistence after partial failure rather than triggering withdrawal.

*Coupling to assessment.* The post-installation sign-off uses the same criterion the framing
named — SIL/SDA per AC 20-165B, ES squitter content, NIC/NACp class — so the mastery target
the technician pursues *is* what the assessment scores. This forecloses the Senko–Miles (2008)
trap where mastery framing lets the learner pursue an interesting sub-topic (e.g., signal
strength on the trace) while the actual assessment scores the fields they neglected.

**K-12 math — fifth-grade fraction-operations summative assessment feedback.**

*Setup.* A fifth grader has just received a returned quiz on adding/subtracting fractions
with unlike denominators. They scored 7/10. The default move in many classrooms is to compare
to peer distribution ("class average was 8.5") or to assign rank-based recognition (top three
get a sticker). Both cues install performance orientation.

*Mastery-approach framing.* The tutor reframes against criterion and prior self: "Last week
you got 4/10 on this same skill — three more correct now. The criterion is finding a
correct common denominator and adding the renamed numerators. Of the three you missed, two
were common-denominator errors and one was a renaming error. Which one do you want to attack
first?" The framing is criterion-referenced, the comparison is to the learner's prior
attempt, the agency is preserved (the learner picks the next move), and the attribution is to
strategy not ability — the four TARGET levers Ames (1992, pp. 263-265) names.

*Coupling to assessment and difficulty.* The next quiz is built on the same observable
criteria the framing named (common denominators, renamed numerators) so the mastery target
matches what is scored — closing the Senko–Miles (2008) gap. The sub-goal ("close the
common-denominator gap by tomorrow") is set at ~70% expected success per Senko and Hulleman
(2013, pp. 514-518), not at the aspirational 10/10 that would predict abandonment.

## Quality signal

The AI tutor knows mastery-approach framing is engaging when (a) on returned-assessment items, learner self-reports of "I want to keep working on this" exceed the rates seen under matched performance-framing conditions by an effect size in the range Van Yperen et al. (2014, pp. 4-9) report for approach versus avoidance goals (small but reliable, |r| ≈ .14), (b) post-failure persistence — measured as time-on-task or attempt-count after an error — does not drop more than 20% relative to the pre-error baseline, and (c) the assessment criterion the framing named matches the criterion the assessment scores (Senko–Miles coupling check). Negative signal: post-feedback withdrawal, ability-attribution language from the learner ("I'm just not good at this"), or selective preparation that ignores tested-but-uninteresting sub-topics.
