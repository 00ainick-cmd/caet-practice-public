# Testing Effect (Retrieval Practice)

## One-line claim

Asking the learner to actively retrieve information from memory produces substantially more durable learning than re-reading or re-studying the same material for the same amount of time.

## Evidence base

The testing effect (also called retrieval practice) is the finding that the act of retrieving information from memory strengthens that memory more than re-exposure to the information does. Roediger and Karpicke's (2006) review in *Perspectives on Psychological Science* synthesized roughly a century of laboratory work — beginning with Abbott (1909) and Gates (1917) — and demonstrated that taking a test on studied material consistently produced better long-term retention than restudying for an equivalent period, even when no feedback was provided on the test. Their companion experimental paper that year (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006, *Psychological Science*) showed that a single retrieval attempt produced 50–80% retention at one week, while restudying produced 30–40% retention over the same delay.

Modern meta-analytic evidence places the effect on solid ground. Adesope, Trevisan, and Sundararajan's (2017) meta-analysis of 118 studies in the *Review of Educational Research* reported a mean Hedge's g of 0.50 favoring practice testing over restudy, with the advantage robust across age groups, materials, and outcome measures. Karpicke and Blunt (2011) extended the finding to inferential transfer: in *Science*, retrieval practice outperformed elaborative concept mapping not only on verbatim recall but on inference questions and conceptual transfer tests. Roediger and Butler's (2011) review in *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* compiled the long-term retention evidence, showing that the gap between tested and restudied material widens — not shrinks — as the retention interval lengthens. Dunlosky et al. (2013) ranked practice testing as one of only two learning techniques (alongside distributed practice) earning the highest "high utility" rating across age, ability, materials, and outcome measures.

The principal boundary condition is element interactivity. Van Gog and Sweller (2015) argued in *Educational Psychology Review* that the testing effect attenuates — and in their reading disappears — when learning materials require integrating many interacting elements simultaneously. The wider field has tempered the strongest version of this claim while preserving its core: subsequent classroom and laboratory work shows the net benefit of retrieval is highest for low-complexity tasks and shrinks (without typically reversing) as task complexity grows. Practical implication: retrieval practice works best after the learner has built a mental model, not during initial encoding of densely interconnected new material.

## When to apply

- **New concept just completed** — Within the same session, after a learner has been exposed to a
  discrete concept, prompt one or more retrieval attempts before moving on. The first retrieval
  should land within minutes of initial exposure, not after the next break.
- **Segment boundary** — At the end of a coherent lesson segment (5–15 minutes of new material),
  pause and ask the learner to recall the key points from memory before any review or summary
  is shown. Free recall here doubles as both retrieval and a comprehension check.
- **Delayed review window opens** — When a spacing schedule (see
  [spaced-retrieval](../01-learning-science/spaced-retrieval.md)) signals it is time to revisit
  material, use retrieval rather than re-presentation as the review modality. The combination of
  retrieval + spacing produces the largest known durable-learning effects in the literature.
- **Pre-assessment check-in** — Before introducing a new topic that builds on prior material,
  prompt retrieval of the prerequisite knowledge to surface gaps and prime encoding of what
  follows.
- **Long-delay retention is the goal** — If the learner needs to recall this in days, weeks, or
  months (certification exam, fieldwork, downstream coursework, on-the-job recall), retrieval is
  the highest-leverage intervention available; the test/restudy gap widens with delay
  (Roediger & Butler, 2011).
- **Inferential transfer is wanted** — Karpicke & Blunt (2011) showed retrieval practice produces
  larger gains than elaborative concept mapping not just on verbatim recall but on inference
  questions; choose retrieval when the downstream criterion involves applying knowledge.

## When NOT to apply

- **Learner is in the initial encoding phase of a complex, high-element-interactivity concept** —
  Forcing retrieval before a stable mental model exists invites guessing and frustration without
  learning gains (Van Gog & Sweller, 2015). Build the model with worked examples or guided
  explanation first; retrieve once the structure is in place.
- **Cognitive load is already saturated** — If the learner is visibly overwhelmed (slow
  responses, errors on basics, expressed frustration, or the pre-assessment is below ~50%
  accuracy on the current segment), adding retrieval demand worsens the load. Reduce extraneous
  load first, retrieve second.
- **Pure motor skill acquisition phase** — Solo verbal retrieval has limited effect on
  procedural motor learning where physical practice and corrective feedback dominate; the
  principle applies to the declarative and conceptual content embedded in motor tasks (e.g.,
  "what torque value goes here?") not to the motor execution itself.
- **Recognition is being substituted for recall** — A multiple-choice item with obvious
  distractors is recognition, not retrieval. The effect requires generative effort; "easy"
  recognition produces a fraction of the benefit (Dunlosky et al., 2013).
- **No feedback channel exists** — Retrieval without feedback locks in errors. If the
  environment cannot provide a correct answer after the attempt (e.g., open-ended generative
  prompt with no expert review), pick a different strategy.

## How to apply

- **Use predict-before-reveal** at the start of new lesson segments (see
  [predict-before-reveal](../04-delivery-patterns/predict-before-reveal.md)) — ask the learner
  to predict, recall, or generate the answer before showing the explanation. This converts
  what would be passive exposure into active retrieval at almost zero time cost.
- **Prefer free recall and short-answer when feasible; multiple-choice with feedback is fine
  when not.** McDermott et al. (2014) found multiple-choice and short-answer roughly equivalent
  in middle and high school classrooms when both were paired with corrective feedback. Without
  feedback, short-answer wins. The decisive variable is generative effort plus feedback, not
  format per se.
- **Provide corrective feedback after every retrieval attempt** — feedback after retrieval
  roughly doubles the testing effect for incorrect answers and protects against error
  consolidation (Roediger & Butler, 2011). Reveal the correct answer; if the retrieval was
  wrong, also surface the mechanism so the learner updates the model, not just the answer.
- **Space the retrieval attempts** — combine with
  [spaced-retrieval](../01-learning-science/spaced-retrieval.md) on an expanding interval
  (e.g., 0 → 1 day → 3 days → 7 days) rather than massing three retrievals inside one session.
  Massed retrieval mostly exercises working memory; spaced retrieval is what consolidates.
- **Make retrieval generative, not transcriptive** — "Explain in your own words why X happens
  before Y" is retrieval; "fill in the blank: X happens before ___" usually is not. See
  [self-explanation](../04-delivery-patterns/self-explanation.md) for prompts that combine
  retrieval with elaboration.
- **Calibrate to retrieval success around 70–85%** — retrieval that is too hard (chronic
  failure) demoralizes and locks in errors; too easy (always right) drops to recognition.
  Adjust prompt difficulty so the learner succeeds most of the time but has to work for it.

## Common misapplications

- **Treating multiple-choice recognition as retrieval** — Recognition is easier than recall and
  produces weaker retention. If a multiple-choice item must be used, write distractors close
  enough to force genuine retrieval rather than process-of-elimination guessing.
- **Retrieving before encoding** — Asking a learner to recall something they have not yet built
  a mental model for produces noise, not learning. Confirm baseline understanding before
  demanding retrieval. (The pretesting effect is a separate, narrower phenomenon — see
  pretesting chapter — and is not a license to skip encoding.)
- **Over-massing retrieval in a single session** — Three retrievals at 0, 30 seconds, and 60
  seconds is mostly working-memory rehearsal. Three retrievals at 0, 1 day, and 7 days is the
  testing effect at full strength. The space between attempts is doing most of the work.
- **Skipping feedback to "save time"** — Unfed retrieval still works for already-correct
  answers but locks in errors for wrong ones. Always close the loop with the right answer,
  even if it adds a few seconds.
- **Using retrieval as a substitute for instruction** — Retrieval is a memory-strengthening
  operation on already-encoded material. It does not replace explanation, modeling, or worked
  examples for novel concepts; it complements them.

## Examples across domains

**Avionics — IFR-6000 ramp test for an ADS-B Out installation.**

*Setup.* A technician has just walked through the procedure for verifying ADS-B Out
compliance using a Mode S transponder ramp tester (e.g., IFR-6000 / T-47). They have read
the procedure once and watched a brief demonstration. Three full re-reads or a single
retrieval-with-feedback are equally expensive in time.

*Retrieval prompt.* Instead of having them re-read, the tutor asks them to close the manual
and answer from memory: "What three Mode S replies are you confirming on the trace, in what
order, and what specifically indicates a failure for each?" The technician retrieves what
they can — typically two of three correctly on a first attempt — and the tutor reveals the
full answer with a one-sentence explanation of the failure mode they missed.

*Spacing.* A second retrieval is scheduled at +24 hours before the technician touches real
hardware on the ramp, and a third at +7 days during the next shop session. The
retrieval-then-feedback sequence beats a third re-read of the same procedure for one-week
retention by roughly d = 0.5 in the meta-analytic average (Adesope et al., 2017).

**K-12 math — fraction operations in a fifth-grade tutoring session.**

*Setup.* A student has just completed a guided lesson on adding fractions with unlike
denominators. They have worked three problems with the tutor, getting the last one right
without prompting. The natural next move is another worked example or a re-read of the
rule; instead, the tutor uses retrieval.

*Retrieval prompt.* "Without looking back, walk me through what you do first when you see
two fractions with different denominators, and explain why that step is necessary." The
student narrates from memory, gets the procedural step right ("find a common denominator")
but stumbles on the rationale. The tutor supplies the missing reason — "you can only add
parts of the same size" — and asks the student to repeat it once in their own words.

*Spacing.* The same retrieval prompt returns at the start of the next session two days
later as the warm-up, then again a week later as a one-question check before introducing
fraction subtraction. Free-recall retrieval with rationale produces both stronger
verbatim recall and better transfer to subtraction problems than a worked-example-only
sequence (Karpicke & Blunt, 2011, on inferential transfer).

## Quality signal

The AI tutor knows retrieval practice is producing learning when retention on retrieval-applied items measured at +24 hours and +7 days exceeds retention on non-retrieved control items by Cohen's d > 0.3 — within the range Adesope et al. (2017) predicts. A weaker but faster signal is in-session retrieval accuracy on a delayed second attempt; if the second-attempt accuracy is not at least 15 percentage points higher than the first, the retrieval prompt is too easy (likely recognition, not recall) or feedback is failing to land.
