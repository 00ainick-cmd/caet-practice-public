Section 4: Wire Harness Fabrication & Testing

Engage

Why should you care about this?

~4 min

Wire harnesses are the central nervous system of an aircraft. Every control signal, every engine parameter, every navigation fix, and every warning light depends on current flowing through a bundle of copper conductors you built with your own hands. When an avionics technician builds a harness incorrectly, the aircraft does not fail loudly on the first flight. It fails quietly, intermittently, on the forty-seventh flight, at night, in weather, when the crew is already task-saturated. That is the cost of a chafed wire, a cold crimp, or a shield that was never bonded.

The NTSB docket is full of accidents traced back to wiring workmanship. Swissair 111 (1998) lost control of the cabin due to arcing from an in-flight entertainment wiring bundle that was not properly separated, clamped, or fused. TWA 800 (1996) center-fuel-tank explosion investigations identified cracked and chafed wires that allowed a spark into a fuel-vapor environment. Every one of these accidents started with a harness built by a technician who thought "close enough" was good enough. It never is.

Explore

Discover the terminology

~8 min

Avionics work has its own vocabulary. Master these terms before procedures, because the oral-board examiner expects precision, not approximations.

Ampacity

The maximum current a conductor can carry continuously without exceeding its temperature rating. Determined by wire gauge, insulation type, and bundle density. A 22-gauge wire alone is rated ~5 A; the same wire in a tight bundle of 15 conductors derates to ~3 A.

Bonding

The intentional electrical connection between two metallic objects to ensure they remain at the same potential, preventing static buildup and providing a low-impedance return path for fault current. Bonding straps use braided copper and are never painted over.

Chafe

Abrasion of wire insulation caused by contact with a structure, another wire, or a moving component. Chafe is the leading cause of in-flight arc faults. Prevent with proper clamping, grommets, and service loops.

Cold Crimp

A crimp that appears complete visually but has not fully compressed the terminal barrel around the wire strands, resulting in a high-resistance connection. Usually caused by undersized die or ratchet not fully cycling. Detectable by pull test and resistance measurement.

Continuity

An unbroken electrical path from one point to another. Measured in ohms; a continuous wire shows a resistance equal to its DC resistance (typically 0.01–0.5 Ω for aircraft harness runs). "OL" or infinite resistance indicates an open conductor.

EMI (Electromagnetic Interference)

Unwanted electrical noise coupled into signal wires from nearby radiating sources (alternator, ignition, radio transmitters). Mitigated by shielded cable, twisted pairs, proper routing, and correct shield termination.

Explain

The theory you need

~12 min

Memorizing steps isn't enough. The evaluator asks why. Here is what you need to understand.

4.2.1 — Wire Types

Aircraft wire is specified by military standard (MIL-W) or aerospace standard (SAE AS). The specification you select governs temperature rating, voltage rating, abrasion resistance, flammability behavior, and mechanical flexibility. Using "general-purpose" hardware-store wire in an aircraft is not merely non-compliant — it is a direct contributor to fatal accidents.

  • MIL-W-22759/16 (PTFE/Tefzel insulation, tin-coated copper) is the most common modern aircraft hookup wire. Rated − 65 °C to +150 °C, 600 V, self-extinguishing.
  • MIL-W-22759/32 and /87 are used in engine compartments where temperatures exceed 200 °C. Do not use /16 in these zones.
  • Kapton (MIL-W-81381) was widely used in older aircraft but is prone to dry-arc tracking when chafed. It is being removed from most platforms.
  • Wire gauge (AWG) determines ampacity. In a bundle, derate per AC 43.13-1B Table 11-6 — a 20-gauge wire alone carries 11 A but only 6.5 A in a 15-conductor bundle at 60 °C ambient.

4.2.2–4.2.3 — Crimp and Shield Termination

A crimp is a gas-tight metal-to-metal connection formed by compressing a terminal barrel around a stripped wire with calibrated force. When done correctly, a crimp is stronger than the wire itself and will outlast soldered joints in a vibration environment. When done incorrectly, it is the single most common source of harness failure.

  • Match the die to the terminal, not the wire: red die for 22–18 AWG terminals, blue for 16–14, yellow for 12–10. Dies are color-coded on the tool head.
  • Use a ratcheting crimper (MS3191 class) that will not release until the full compression cycle completes. Plier-type crimpers are prohibited for aircraft work.
  • Strip length matters: too short and strands do not enter the barrel; too long and bare strands extend past the terminal, risking a short. Follow the terminal manufacturer's strip chart — usually printed on the terminal package.
  • A proper crimp shows the insulation butted against the insulation grip with strands visible through the inspection hole. The conductor barrel should show a clean hex impression with no cracks.

4.2.8–4.2.9 — Multimeter and Megohmmeter

Two instruments verify a harness is good. They measure different things and are not interchangeable. A multimeter finds opens and shorts at low voltage. A megger finds insulation breakdown paths that only appear at operating voltage or higher. Both are required before a harness is released to service.

  • Digital multimeter (DMM) in continuity/low-ohm mode applies < 1 V across the conductor. Acceptable readings for aircraft runs are < 0.5 Ω end-to-end; anything above 1 Ω on a short run indicates a bad crimp or corroded terminal.
  • Use the DMM's 4-wire (Kelvin) mode for low-resistance measurements when available, to eliminate test-lead resistance from the reading.
  • A megohmmeter applies 250–1000 V DC across insulation while measuring leakage current. Typical aircraft acceptance: > 100 MΩ between any two conductors and between any conductor and ground.
  • Before meggering: disconnect both ends of the harness from any avionics. A 500 V megger WILL destroy most transistorized equipment. This is the single most common way technicians create additional write-ups.

Apply it to a scenario

~15 min
Scenario: You're on day one of your practical exam. The evaluator hands you a wire list and says, "Build me a three-conductor shielded harness, 24 inches long, ring-terminated on both ends. You have 45 minutes. Questions before you start?" What do you ask — and then what do you do?

Task focus: Task 4.4.1 — Harness Fabrication

Read the procedure once all the way through before you touch a tool. Note how each step has a verification built in — the procedure is self-checking.

  1. Read the wire list and routing diagram before you cut anything. Verify you have the correct wire gauges, terminals, and connectors staged at your bench.
  2. Measure the routing path using a mock-up harness, a string, or a measuring tape following the actual routing. Add 10–15% for bend radius and service loops.
  3. Cut each wire to length. Label both ends immediately with a heat-shrink wire marker showing the wire number from the diagram. Never cut multiple wires and label later — you will cross them up.
  4. Strip each end to the terminal manufacturer's specified length. Inspect for nicked strands (more than 3 nicked strands = re-strip).
  5. Terminate each end with the correct terminal and correct-color die. Inspect each crimp visually and with a pull test (4 lb minimum for 22 AWG, 8 lb for 20 AWG, 16 lb for 18 AWG).
