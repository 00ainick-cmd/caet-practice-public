Engage

Why should you care about this?

~4 min

Every time a pilot rolls into a turn in IMC, they trust that a small box of MEMS sensors and a magnetometer mounted somewhere in the airframe is telling them the truth about which way is up and which way is north. AHRS replaced the spinning-mass attitude gyro and the slaved remote gyro compass that dominated panels for fifty years, and it did so because solid-state sensors are more reliable, lighter, and cheaper. But the trade is that AHRS errors are no longer mechanical and obvious (a tumbled gyro you can hear and see) — they are computational and silent. A miscompensated magnetometer can drive a GPSS autopilot into a 20-degree heading error over a ten-minute leg without ever raising a flag. That is the failure mode you are installing against.

The accident record is unforgiving. The B-2 Spirit crash at Andersen AFB in 2008 was traced to moisture in three of the aircraft's 24 port-transducer units skewing the air data feeding the flight control system — a calibration and initialization failure in the sensor suite that left the aircraft believing it was in a different attitude than it actually was. Air France 447 (2009), while primarily a pitot-icing and crew-response accident, exposed how rapidly spatial disorientation sets in when the displayed attitude disagrees with the seat-of-the-pants feel, and how badly line pilots handle an unreliable attitude display. General aviation CFIT accidents under the heading 'loss of control — IMC' routinely involve pilots following a heading bug that was three to eight degrees off because the magnetometer was mounted too close to a newly installed LED landing light.

Explore

Discover the terminology

~8 min

Avionics work has its own vocabulary. Master these terms before procedures, because the oral-board examiner expects precision, not approximations.

AHRS (Attitude & Heading Reference System)

A solid-state sensor package that outputs aircraft attitude (pitch, roll) and magnetic heading to the PFD, autopilot, flight director, and other consumers. Replaces mechanical vertical gyro, directional gyro, and slaved remote compass. Typical units: Garmin GRS 77/GRS 79, Collins AHC-3000, Honeywell AH-2100, Aspen EA100, Sandia SAI-340.

MEMS Gyroscope

Micro-Electro-Mechanical System rate sensor that uses the Coriolis force on a vibrating silicon mass to detect angular rate about an axis. No moving macro parts. Typical consumer-grade drift: 10-50 degrees per hour; tactical-grade: 1-10 degrees per hour; navigation-grade: <0.1 degrees per hour. Drift is corrected by sensor fusion with accelerometers, magnetometer, and GPS.

Ring Laser Gyro (RLG)

Optical rotation sensor that splits a laser beam in two counter-rotating paths around a closed cavity; the Sagnac effect produces a frequency difference proportional to rotation rate. Drift of 0.01 degrees per hour is typical. Found in IRS/INS on transport aircraft (Honeywell Laseref series). Not common in GA AHRS due to cost and size.

Fiber Optic Gyro (FOG)

Similar Sagnac-effect optical gyro using a coiled fiber to extend the optical path. Performance between MEMS and RLG, with no moving parts and good shock tolerance. Used in mid-tier AHRS and some tactical IRS (Northrop LN-100, Honeywell HG1700 family).

IMU (Inertial Measurement Unit)

Physical sensor block containing 3-axis gyros and 3-axis accelerometers (sometimes a magnetometer). An IMU by itself does not output attitude — it outputs raw rates and accelerations. An AHRS is an IMU plus the software to fuse those signals into stable attitude and heading.

IRS vs AHRS

An Inertial Reference System (IRS) outputs position, velocity, and attitude — a full navigation solution using navigation-grade gyros and accelerometers, typically requiring a stationary gyrocompassing alignment. An AHRS outputs only attitude and heading (no position or velocity) using lower-grade sensors and accepts external GPS aiding. AHRS is standard in GA and regional aircraft; IRS is standard in transport aircraft.

Explain

The theory you need

~12 min

Memorizing steps isn't enough. The evaluator asks why. Here is what you need to understand.

AHRS Architecture

An AHRS is not a sensor — it is a computer running a sensor-fusion algorithm (typically an extended Kalman filter) that combines the outputs of several imperfect sensors into one attitude and heading solution that is better than any single sensor. Understanding how the filter weights its inputs is how you diagnose an AHRS that is displaying something wrong.

  • A representative modern AHRS (Garmin GRS 77/79, Collins AHC-3000, Honeywell AH-2100, Aspen EA100) is a Line Replaceable Unit that contains three MEMS rate gyros, three MEMS accelerometers, and an internal processor. The magnetometer is typically a separate remote sensor (Garmin GMU 44B, Aspen EA100 remote mag) mounted away from the AHRS box in a magnetically clean area.
  • The Kalman filter treats the gyros as short-term truth and the accelerometers/magnetometer/GPS as long-term truth. During a turn, the gyros carry the attitude solution; during level flight, the accelerometers pull the solution back toward the gravity vector and the magnetometer pulls heading toward magnetic north.
  • GPS aiding (per RTCA DO-334 and RTCA DO-229 interoperability expectations) provides a ground-track reference that the filter uses to detect and correct magnetometer errors in flight. A modern installation without GPS aiding is degraded by design — verify the GPS feed is active during your post-install functional test.
  • Pitch and roll accuracy on certified AHRS (TSO-C4c replacement for older gyro horizons, or TSO-C6e-equivalent integrated systems) is typically better than +/-1 degree under non-accelerated conditions and +/-2 degrees during maneuvering. Heading accuracy post-compensation is typically +/-1 to 2 degrees per TSO-C6e for magnetic heading instruments.

Sensor Technologies

You will not install a Ring Laser Gyro on a Cessna 172. But you will be asked in an oral why the King Air has a MEMS AHRS and the G650 has a fiber-optic IRS, and the answer is about drift, cost, and what the aircraft needs to know without external aiding.

  • MEMS gyros use a vibrating silicon tuning fork; the Coriolis force on the vibrating mass during rotation is sensed capacitively. Consumer-grade drift is 10-50 deg/hr; tactical-grade MEMS used in aviation AHRS is typically 1-10 deg/hr. This drift is why MEMS AHRS requires continuous external aiding (accelerometer gravity reference, magnetometer heading reference, GPS ground-track reference).
  • Ring Laser Gyros (RLG) — Honeywell Laseref IRS family (GG1320 gyro) — achieve drift of 0.01 deg/hr, good enough for unaided inertial navigation for many hours. They use a helium-neon laser path around a triangular cavity and detect rotation via the Sagnac frequency shift. Expensive, heavy, and needing dithering to avoid 'lock-in' at low rates.
  • Fiber Optic Gyros (FOG) — Honeywell HG1700 family, Northrop LN-200 — use a coiled optical fiber for the same Sagnac effect in a smaller, more shock-tolerant package. Drift of 0.1-1 deg/hr places them between MEMS and RLG. Common in mid-tier AHRS and tactical IMU applications.
  • Accelerometers in modern AHRS are typically MEMS pendulous/capacitive types with bias stability in the milli-g range. They provide the long-term 'down' reference during straight-and-level flight and contribute to coordinated-turn and slip/skid indications.

AHRS Failure Modes

When an AHRS fails, the pilot needs to know within seconds, because a slowly-drifting attitude display is more dangerous than a failed one. The failure annunciations and the logic behind them are a required-knowledge area on the oral.

  • Complete failure: PFD attitude and/or heading presents a Red X or 'ATT FAIL' / 'HDG FAIL' annunciation. Pilot transitions to standby attitude indicator (an independent electric or mechanical unit per 14 CFR 91.205) and magnetic compass. The autopilot typically disengages automatically.
  • Disagreement between dual AHRS: in aircraft with dual AHRS (typical in Part 23 Level 3/4 aircraft and most Part 25), a 'ATT MISCOMPARE' or 'HDG MISCOMPARE' annunciation illuminates when the two sources disagree beyond a threshold (typically 6 degrees attitude, 6 degrees heading). Pilot must determine which is correct — cross-check with standby indicators and GPS ground track.
  • GPS aiding loss: annunciated as 'GPS AID LOST' or 'AHRS DEGRADED'. The system continues to operate but heading accuracy may gradually degrade. Not an immediate emergency but the pilot should note the time to estimate compounding error.
  • Magnetometer out of tolerance: annunciated as 'MAG INVALID' or 'HDG FAIL'. This can happen during start (compensation data corrupted) or in flight (strong nearby field, e.g. ramp-level ground equipment). The AHRS reverts to gyro-only heading, which will drift without magnetic reference.

Dual AHRS and Redundancy

Dual AHRS is not redundancy for redundancy's sake. It is how the system verifies itself in flight. When you install or troubleshoot dual AHRS, you must understand the reversion logic and the source-switching that keeps the pilot in the loop.

  • Each AHRS (AHRS 1, AHRS 2) operates independently with its own magnetometer, its own GPS feed, and its own data bus (typically ARINC 429 to each PFD plus a crossfeed channel for comparison).
  • The pilot's PFD normally displays AHRS 1; the copilot's displays AHRS 2. A reversion switch (mechanical or soft-key) allows either pilot to select the opposite side if their side fails or disagrees. Documented reversion procedure is in the AFM/POH supplement for the installed AHRS.
  • The autopilot must be fed a single, verified attitude source. On dual-AHRS systems, the autopilot typically follows the pilot's PFD-selected source; some installations allow autopilot-specific selection. Know the aircraft's architecture before signing off an installation.
  • The standby attitude indicator (SAI, e.g. Mid-Continent MD302, Sandia SAI-340) is a self-contained independent AHRS/battery unit that is NOT fed from the main AHRS. It operates on ship's DC power with a battery backup of typically 1 hour. Required per 14 CFR 91.205(d) for IFR aircraft.
