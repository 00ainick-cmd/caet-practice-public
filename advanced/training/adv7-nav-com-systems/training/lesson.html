Engage

Why should you care about this?

~4 min

Navigation accuracy is not a convenience feature; it is a safety system. A pilot trusts the needle because they have to. In IMC at minimums, with 200 feet of ceiling and half a mile of visibility, the glideslope indicator and the localizer centerline are the only things between a controlled arrival on pavement and a CFIT accident in the trees short of the threshold. When an avionics technician signs off a VOR check, tunes in an ILS, or installs a GPS/SBAS navigator, they are guaranteeing — in writing — that the needle the pilot follows actually points where the aircraft is going. That is not a paperwork exercise. That is a life-safety promise.

The NTSB and ICAO accident record is unambiguous on this point. Korean Air 801 (Guam, 1997) flew a serviceable airplane into Nimitz Hill at night because the crew believed they were tracking a functioning glideslope; the ground-based glideslope transmitter was actually out of service for maintenance, a fact that was NOTAM'd but missed, and the captain's mental model of their vertical position was wrong. American Airlines 965 (Cali, 1995) descended into terrain after an FMS waypoint selection error — the crew entered 'R' expecting Rozo NDB and the FMS autoselected Romeo, 132 nautical miles away, turning the aircraft toward mountains. More recently, GPS spoofing incidents in the Middle East (2023–2024) have demonstrated that pilots cannot blindly trust a single nav source, even a modern WAAS-equipped one. In every case, the avionics worked exactly as designed; the chain broke somewhere between the ground station, the antenna, the receiver, and the pilot's understanding of what the needle meant.

Discover the terminology

~8 min

Avionics work has its own vocabulary. Master these terms before procedures, because the oral-board examiner expects precision, not approximations.

VOR (VHF Omni-Directional Range)

Ground-based nav aid transmitting on 108.00–117.95 MHz (even 100 kHz for ILS localizer, odd 50 kHz carriers for VOR in the 108–112 band, and continuous 50 kHz spacing above 112 MHz). Provides magnetic bearing FROM the station in 360 radials. Standard VOR uses a rotating directional cardioid plus omni reference; Doppler VOR (DVOR) synthesizes the same phase relationship electronically to reduce siting errors near terrain.

Localizer (LOC)

Lateral component of ILS. Transmits a dual-sideband signal on 108.10–111.95 MHz (odd-tenths only, e.g., 110.30, not 110.20). A 90 Hz modulation dominates on one side of the runway centerline, 150 Hz on the other. The aircraft receiver produces a difference-in-depth-of-modulation (DDM) signal that drives the CDI. Full-scale deflection is approximately ±2.5° at the threshold, ±700 feet at the outer marker.

Glideslope (GS)

Vertical component of ILS. Transmits on 329.3–335.0 MHz in UHF, paired automatically with the localizer frequency in a tuning table (the pilot tunes only the LOC). Typical glidepath angle is 3.00°. Full-scale deflection is ±0.7°, or about ±485 feet at the outer marker. TSO-C36 governs airborne glideslope receivers.

Marker Beacons (OM/MM/IM)

75 MHz vertical fan markers defining fixes along the ILS final approach course. Outer Marker (OM) at ~4–7 nm, blue light, 400 Hz tone, 2 dashes/sec. Middle Marker (MM) at ~0.5–0.8 nm, amber light, 1300 Hz tone, alternating dots/dashes. Inner Marker (IM) for Cat II/III only, white light, 3000 Hz tone, 6 dots/sec. Largely being replaced by DME or GPS fixes but still present on legacy approaches.

GPS L1/L2/L5

GPS operates on multiple frequencies. L1 = 1575.42 MHz, carries the C/A civilian code and P(Y) military code, used by all civil receivers. L2 = 1227.60 MHz, military P(Y) and civil L2C. L5 = 1176.45 MHz, protected aviation-safety band, used by modern dual-frequency SBAS receivers to resist interference and ionospheric error.

WAAS / SBAS

Wide Area Augmentation System (U.S.) is one implementation of SBAS (Satellite-Based Augmentation System, ICAO term). Ground reference stations measure GPS errors, a master station computes differential corrections and integrity data, and geostationary satellites broadcast those corrections to airborne receivers on L1 (and L5 in newer installations). RTCA DO-229D specifies latency <6 seconds end-to-end. Improves GPS horizontal accuracy from ~3–7 m to ~1–2 m with vertical integrity sufficient for LPV approaches.

The theory you need

~12 min

Memorizing steps isn't enough. The evaluator asks why. Here is what you need to understand.

6.2.1 — VOR Operation

A VOR station radiates two signals on the same carrier: a 30 Hz FM reference that is the same in every direction, and a 30 Hz AM variable whose phase rotates at 30 revolutions per second (the cardioid pattern sweeping around). The aircraft's receiver compares the phase of the two signals. At magnetic north of the station, they are in phase. Everywhere else, the phase difference equals the magnetic bearing from the station. The receiver converts that phase difference into a radial readout, drives the CDI relative to the pilot-selected OBS course, and sets the TO/FROM flag based on whether the aircraft is closer to the reciprocal or the selected course. The technician's job is to understand this well enough to explain why a VOR is more susceptible to siting errors than DVOR, why CDI deflection is angular rather than linear, and why the published accuracy (±4°) translates to larger and larger lateral errors the farther you fly from the station.

  • Frequency band 108.00–117.95 MHz VHF. In the 108.00–111.95 portion, even-tenth frequencies are ILS localizers and odd-tenth are VORs; above 112.00, all are VORs.
  • TSO-C34 (conventional VOR receiver) and TSO-C40 (airborne VOR) are the governing airworthiness standards; modern combined nav receivers are certified under later integrated TSOs but the underlying VOR function still traces to C34/C40 performance.
  • Standard VOR has a cardioid radiation pattern that rotates mechanically or electrically at 30 Hz. Doppler VOR (DVOR) uses a ring of ~50 Alford-loop antennas switched electronically to synthesize Doppler shift, reducing siting error over uneven terrain. To the airborne receiver, both look identical.
  • Accuracy specification: the ground station is held to ±1° at the antenna. The airborne receiver has its own error (±4° typical). The total system budget for IFR use per 14 CFR 91.171 is ±4° from a VOT or ground checkpoint, ±6° airborne, or ±4° between two VORs on a dual check.

6.2.2 — ILS Operation

The Instrument Landing System is the classical precision approach aid — three independent signals that together define a 3-dimensional path to the runway. The localizer provides lateral guidance via two overlapping VHF lobes modulated at 90 Hz and 150 Hz; the receiver computes Difference in Depth of Modulation (DDM) and deflects the CDI left or right. The glideslope works identically in UHF for the vertical path. Marker beacons (75 MHz) define along-track fixes; many ILS approaches now use DME or RNAV-derived fixes instead. The technician must understand the signal structure well enough to troubleshoot a flag indication, verify proper frequency pairing, confirm antenna installation (particularly the glideslope antenna's clear radome path), and explain to a pilot why a tailwind on a Cat I ILS is managed differently than on an RNAV LPV. ICAO Annex 10 Vol 1 and FAA Order 8260 series define the ground-system specs; TSO-C34 (LOC) and TSO-C36 (GS) govern the airborne receivers.

  • Localizer: 108.10–111.95 MHz, odd-tenth frequencies only (110.10, 110.30, 110.50… never 110.20). Forty channels total in the band.
  • Glideslope: 329.3–335.0 MHz UHF. Paired automatically — tuning the localizer frequency also tunes the glideslope; the pilot never tunes GS separately. The pairing table is part of the receiver firmware and must survive software updates.
  • Localizer CDI: full-scale ±2.5° at the runway threshold (roughly ±350 ft at threshold; ±700 ft at the OM about 5 nm out). This is four times more sensitive than VOR (±10°). Installers must verify CDI scaling changes correctly when the LOC frequency is tuned — a nav radio stuck in VOR sensitivity on an ILS frequency is a classic installation failure.
  • Glideslope angle: typically 3.00°, occasionally 2.50° (flat approach for terrain) or up to 6° (LDA with GS or special steep approach). Full-scale GS deflection ±0.7°; one dot = ~0.35°. At 200 ft DH on a 3° GS, one dot high/low = ~50 ft vertical error.

6.2.3 — GPS/GNSS and WAAS

GPS receivers have gone through three certification generations that a working technician must distinguish. TSO-C129a (legacy panel-mount GPS, mid-1990s through early 2000s: Garmin GNS 430/530, KLN 94, GX-60) provides en-route, terminal, and non-precision approach LNAV capability with RAIM for integrity. TSO-C145d/C146d (modern SBAS receivers: Garmin GTN 650/750/Xi, GNX 375, GNC-355, Avidyne IFD, most G1000/G3000/Pro Line panels) adds WAAS/SBAS corrections enabling LPV and LNAV/VNAV approaches down to 200-ft DH. The newest TSO-C196 and C199 cover dual-frequency L1/L5 receivers designed to resist ionospheric error and GPS interference. For the technician, the certification generation of the installed box determines what approaches the aircraft is legal to fly, what database is required, what antenna is compatible, and what RAIM/SBAS prediction the pilot must perform before IFR flight. That is not paperwork trivia; getting it wrong means signing off an aircraft to fly an approach it was never certified for.

  • GPS L1 C/A: 1575.42 MHz, 1.023 Mbps code, civilian. L2C: 1227.60 MHz, civilian (second frequency, not available on most GA installations). L5: 1176.45 MHz, protected aviation band, in TSO-C196 dual-frequency receivers. Galileo, GLONASS, BeiDou broadcast on overlapping bands and modern multi-constellation receivers (GTN Xi, GNX series) use them all.
  • GPS requires four satellites for a 3D position fix: three to solve for latitude/longitude/altitude, one more to solve for receiver clock bias. With five, RAIM is possible; with six, FDE.
  • SBAS broadcast comes from geostationary satellites (GPS PRN 131, 133, 135, 138 are WAAS; similar slots for EGNOS, MSAS, etc.). Corrections are encoded per RTCA DO-229D with end-to-end latency under 6 seconds. A modern WAAS receiver uses SBAS data to compute an 'HPL' and 'VPL' (horizontal/vertical protection level) that must stay below the approach-phase 'HAL/VAL' alarm limits or the approach is downgraded in real time.
  • The aviation databases: Jeppesen NavData, Garmin Aviation Database, LIDO — published on a 28-day AIRAC cycle. An IFR-certified GPS under AC 90-100A (RNAV) and AC 90-107 (LPV) requires a current database for enroute, terminal, and approach operations. VFR use with expired database is legal but cautioned.

6.2.4 — CDI Scaling Differences

One CDI needle. Three different sensitivities. The same one-dot deflection means very different real-world lateral errors depending on what the nav radio is tracking. This is the single most common source of pilot confusion and one of the most common installation sign-off items the technician must verify: the CDI changes sensitivity automatically and correctly when the nav source changes. If you install a GPS/NAV/COM without verifying the CDI transitions from ±5° en-route GPS to ±1° terminal to angular LPV scaling, you have handed the pilot a needle that lies at exactly the wrong moment.

  • VOR: angular, ±10° full scale, 5 dots each side, 2° per dot. Full-scale deflection at 60 nm is about 10 nm of linear offset; at 10 nm is 1.7 nm.
  • ILS localizer: angular, ±2.5° full scale, 5 dots each side, 0.5° per dot. One dot at the OM (5 nm out) = ~140 ft lateral; at threshold = ~35 ft. Four times more sensitive than VOR.
  • GPS en-route mode: linear, ±5 nm full scale (older TSO-C129a) or ±2 nm (typical modern C145 default). Sensitivity transitions to terminal (±1 nm) within 30 nm of destination airport.
  • GPS approach mode (LNAV): linear, ±0.3 nm at the FAF, tapering along the approach. Transitions automatically when the receiver detects the procedure is active and the aircraft is within the FAF turn.

Task focus: Task 6.4.1 — VOR Tuning and Interpretation

Read the procedure once all the way through before you touch a tool. Note how each step has a verification built in — the procedure is self-checking.

  1. Verify the nav radio is powered and self-test completed (no red X or flag on the CDI and HSI).
  2. Select a published VOR from the sectional or IFR chart. Confirm frequency, location, and service volume.
  3. Tune the VOR frequency on the nav radio. On combined boxes (GTN 650/750, GNC-255, KX-155), use the nav-side frequency knob and verify the active frequency display.
  4. Identify the station: listen for the Morse code ident on the audio panel's NAV monitor. Three letters, continuous repetition. If you hear 'TEST' or no ident at all, the station is unmonitored and unusable for IFR.
  5. Rotate the OBS (Omni-Bearing Selector) until the CDI needle centers and the TO/FROM flag shows 'TO' (or 'FROM'). Read the selected course from the OBS index.

Trap to avoid Skipping the ident and trusting a signal from a station that is actually out of service or on test transmission. Missing ident = unusable signal.
